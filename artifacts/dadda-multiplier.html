Artifact 4 — 6-Bit Dadda-Optimized Multiplier

An ENEE640 (VLSI) project comparing multiplier architectures and delivering a static CMOS 6-bit Dadda tree with Verilog verification, schematic-driven layout, and a measured focus on worst-case delay and power.

Architecture

Highlights

  • Architecture choice: Dadda over Wallace and Vedic-style options for a better trade-off between reduction depth and implementation complexity in CMOS; fewer reduction stages than a comparable Wallace tree in this design space.
  • Hierarchical design: Partial-product generation (AND array), dedicated Dadda reduction stages using half- and full-adders, and a final summation stage partitioned for debug and optimization.
  • Final adder experiment: A carry-lookahead adder was prototyped for the last 10-bit combine, but measured delay and power favored a ripple-carry adder in this layout — a reminder that “theoretically faster” does not always win once wires and fan-out are real.
  • Verification: Initial Verilog modeling and simulation to prove functional correctness before committing to full custom schematic flow.
  • Motivation: Multipliers are a recurring bottleneck in neural-network accelerators; the report frames the design as a building block for that class of system.

Context & reflection

This was my ENEE640 Exam 1 deliverable: not just drawing a multiplier, but defending why this topology, how each block maps to transistors, and what the delay and power measurements say after optimization. The most valuable part of the write-up is the honest comparison between the CLA and RCA for the final combine — it forced me to reconcile textbook latency models with what the extracted layout actually did. That habit of trusting the meter over the napkin sketch is the same one I rely on in synthesis-heavy projects like the Tomasulo CPU and the standard-cell placer.
